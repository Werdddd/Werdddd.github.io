What are the Sustainable Development Goals?
The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), also known as the Global Goals, were adopted by the United Nations in 2015 as a universal call to action to end poverty, protect the planet, and ensure that by 2030 all people enjoy peace and prosperity.

The 17 SDGs are integrated—they recognize that action in one area will affect outcomes in others, and that development must balance social, economic and environmental sustainability.

Countries have committed to prioritize progress for those who're furthest behind. The SDGs are designed to end poverty, hunger, AIDS, and discrimination against women and girls.

The creativity, knowhow, technology and financial resources from all of society is necessary to achieve the SDGs in every context.

History

  • June 1992
    Agenda 21 Initiation
    The Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, led to the adoption of Agenda 21 by over 178 countries, a global action plan for sustainable development.
  • September 2000
    Millennium Development Goals
    The Millennium Summit in New York resulted in the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs), aimed at reducing extreme poverty by 2015.
  • June 2012
    Rio+20 and SDG Foundation
    At the Rio+20 Conference in Brazil, Member States adopted "The Future We Want," initiating the development of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).
  • September 2015
    Adoption of the 2030 Agenda
    The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, including 17 SDGs, was adopted at the UN Sustainable Development Summit in New York, marking a milestone in global policy.
  • Ongoing
    SDG Review and Support
    The High-level Political Forum on Sustainable Development serves as the central platform for reviewing the SDGs, supported by the UN Department of Economic and Social Affairs.
